# Mojibun - Course Overview

## Course Overview (10-unit roadmap + longer future build)

This overview should answer two questions at once:

1. What are the first 10 units in the app?
2. How does the course continue after that, so we make good pacing decisions now?

The research suggests that beginner Japanese should be planned in broad phases:

- **Phase 1: Survival basics** — greetings, yes/no, self-intro, demonstratives
- **Phase 2: People & places** — family, common objects, places, food/drink
- **Phase 3: Daily life** — time, days, basic verbs, common adjectives
- **Phase 4: Getting around** — transport, directions, shopping, weather
- **Phase 5: Expanding communication** — hobbies, body/health, colors, counters

Our first 10 units should complete Phase 1 and establish the foundation for Phase 2 and early Phase 3.

### First 10 units

These are the first 10 units we plan around. The unit names should stay close to the current app labels, while the descriptions below clarify the intended teaching scope.

```
Course V2 Roadmap: Units 1-10
├── Unit 1: Hello! (5 lessons) — shortest, highest-confidence greetings only
├── Unit 2: Please & Thank You (5 lessons) — polite phrases, yes/no, basic social survival
├── Unit 3: I Am... (6 lessons) — わたし, names, student/teacher, は, です, か
├── Unit 4: What Is This? (6 lessons) — これ / それ / あれ, なん, first objects
├── Unit 5: My Things (6 lessons) — この / その / あの, の possession, more objects
├── Unit 6: Also & Where? (6 lessons) — も, ここ / そこ / あそこ / どこ, どれ / どの
├── Unit 7: People & Family (6 lessons) — family words, person words, personal-world vocabulary
├── Unit 8: Numbers (6 lessons) — 1-10, basic quantity, age, prices, first counting feel
├── Unit 9: Time & Days (6 lessons) — today/tomorrow, morning/night, days, clock time, daily rhythm
└── Unit 10: Places (6 lessons) — school/home/station/store, existence, first real go/come/location sentences
```

### Why these first 10 units

These 10 units cover the beginner ground that research says should come first:

- greetings and essential phrases
- self-introduction
- demonstratives and object identification
- possession and location
- people and family
- numbers and counting
- time and daily routine
- places and moving between them

This keeps the early course concrete and highly visual before expanding into heavier verb systems and more abstract grammar.

### Longer future build after Unit 10

After the first 10 units, the course should keep building in the same order suggested by the research rather than jumping randomly.

### Grammar build across the roadmap

The first 10 units should not try to cover everything. Instead, grammar should be introduced in stages that roughly match the unit roadmap.

#### Stage A: Units 1-6

**Role:** core noun-based beginner Japanese, where learners can identify things, introduce themselves, ask basic questions, and talk about possession and location without relying on full verb systems yet.

**Likely introduced here:**

- です
- は
- か
- の
- も
- これ / それ / あれ
- この / その / あの
- ここ / そこ / あそこ / どこ
- どれ / どの
- じゃないです

**Why here:** these are the smallest, most reusable building blocks for true beginners and support the current concrete-object lesson design.

#### Stage B: Units 7-10

**Role:** widen the learner’s world from objects into people, numbers, time, places, and basic existence/description, while beginning the transition toward real everyday sentence use.

**Likely introduced here:**

- い-adjectives
- な-adjectives
- が
- ある / いる
- に (time / location)
- で (location of action)
- first polite verb exposure in a very limited set
- very high-frequency verbs such as 行きます / 来ます in tightly controlled contexts

**Why here:** these grammar points become useful once the learner already knows people, places, numbers, and time vocabulary. Without a small verb bridge here, the course risks staying in noun-labeling mode for too long.

#### Stage C: Units 11-14

**Role:** expand the polite verb system and make the course feel like “real daily Japanese.”

**Likely introduced here:**

- ます form
- を
- に / へ (direction)
- verb present / future use
- verb negative
- verb past

**Why here:** this is the point where eating, drinking, going, coming, seeing, and doing become central enough to justify verb-heavy lessons. This stage should deepen verbs, not be the learner’s very first encounter with them.

#### Stage D: Units 15-18

**Role:** connect ideas and make practical public-life communication possible.

**Likely introduced here:**

- と
- や
- から
- けど / けれども
- ので

**Why here:** shopping, directions, transport, and weather naturally create situations where learners need to connect nouns, reasons, and short clauses.

#### Stage E: Units 19-22

**Role:** move from describing and stating into asking, requesting, allowing, and prohibiting.

**Likely introduced here:**

- て-form
- てください
- てもいい
- てはいけない

**Why here:** these are powerful but heavier grammar patterns and should come after the learner is comfortable with basic verbs and daily-life situations.

#### Stage F: Units 23-24 and beyond

**Role:** push toward more natural beginner fluency and richer conversation.

**Likely introduced here:**

- ている
- たい
- ほしい
- ましょう / ましょうか
- ませんか
- たことがある
- comparisons (より, ほうが, いちばん)

**Why here:** these patterns are highly useful, but they make more sense once the learner already has a stable vocabulary base, polite verbs, and connected sentences.

### Counters note

Counters should be split into two moments:

- **Early lexical exposure:** small amounts, age, and quantity can appear earlier as vocabulary or fixed expressions
- **Real counter-system teaching:** Japanese-style counter patterns such as ～つ, ～人, ～本, ～枚 should stay later, when learners already have stronger number control and sentence confidence

This keeps early number lessons useful without overloading learners with one of the more uniquely Japanese systems too soon.

### Planning implications for what we teach now

- Units 1-2 should stay extremely gentle and avoid long, visually dense phrases unless they are essential.
- The first 10 units should prioritize concrete nouns, short phrases, and high-confidence sentence patterns.
- We should delay long cultural set phrases, shopping expressions, and verb-heavy content until the learner has a stronger base.
- Units 7-10 should feel like a natural widening of the same beginner world, not a sudden jump in difficulty.
- Units 8-10 should be planned together: numbers support time and prices, while time and places support the first truly useful verb/location sentences.
- Units 9-10 should clearly deliver the first real sentence-utility payoff, not just more labeling.
- Every new unit now should be checked against the longer roadmap: if a word or grammar point belongs to Phase 3 or 4, it should usually not appear in Unit 1 or 2.

**Old structure:** 2 units, 16 lessons, ~46 words, ~10 grammar points
**Current structured plan:** 10-unit early roadmap, with future phases already defined beyond Unit 10
